Porter's Five Forces Framework

Force Score (1-10) Rationale
Threat of New Entrants 3/10
(Low)		 Industry Dynamic: Regulatory requirements (capital, licensing, compliance) make de novo bank formation costly ($50M+ minimum capital). Post-2008 financial crisis regulations (Dodd-Frank, Basel III) raised compliance costs, effectively barring new entrants. Digital-only banks (e.g., SoFi, Chime) compete in consumer banking but lack commercial banking expertise.

For CATY: Specialized Asian-American focus and cross-border expertise create additional moat vs potential entrants. Relationship-based lending model requires decades of community trust.
Bargaining Power of Suppliers 6/10
(Moderate-High)		 Industry Dynamic: Depositors hold increasing power: In current rate environment, deposit competition has intensified as customers are more rate-sensitive post-2022 Fed hiking cycle. High-yield savings accounts from online banks (4-5% APY) pressure traditional banks' funding costs.

For CATY: Sticky deposit base (50%+ noninterest-bearing demand deposits pre-2022) provides cushion, but NIM compression risk exists if Fed cuts rates and deposit repricing lags. Commercial depositors (businesses) have higher switching costs than retail.
Bargaining Power of Buyers 5/10
(Moderate)		 Industry Dynamic: Borrowers have options but relationships matter: Commercial borrowers can access capital from regional banks, national banks (JPM, BofA), credit unions, and non-bank lenders. Price-sensitive borrowers exert pressure on loan spreads. However, relationship banking (treasury management, FX services, wealth advisory) increases switching costs.

For CATY: Specialized CRE and cross-border trade finance expertise reduces commoditization. Asian-American business owners often prefer culturally-aligned banks with bilingual services, reducing buyer power.
Threat of Substitutes 6/10
(Moderate-High)		 Industry Dynamic: Alternative financing sources emerging: Private credit/direct lending funds (Ares, Apollo) compete for middle-market C&I loans. Fintech lenders (Brex, Ramp) serve small business working capital. Commercial mortgage REITs compete for CRE financing. Payment processors (Stripe, Square) disintermediate banks in transaction banking.

For CATY: Deposit-taking franchise (core FDIC-insured deposits) remains durable moat that non-banks cannot replicate. However, loan portfolio faces substitution risk, particularly in commoditized segments (small business term loans, residential mortgages).
Competitive Rivalry 7/10
(High)		 Industry Dynamic: California banking market is highly fragmented with national banks (Wells Fargo, JPM, BofA), superregionals (US Bank, PNC), regional banks (WaFed, PacWest), and credit unions all competing for deposits and loans. Low industry growth (loan growth 3-5% CAGR) intensifies competition for market share.

For CATY: Direct competitors include East West Bancorp (EWBC, $13B mkt cap leader), Hope Bancorp (HOPE), Hanmi Financial (HAFC), and other Asian-American banks. Differentiation through cultural alignment and cross-border expertise is critical. Price competition on commodity products (residential mortgages, basic checking) remains intense.
